Development of a Human-Aware Navigation Framework for Autonomous Mobile Robots

A fuzzy logic and potential field-based motion planning framework prioritizing human safety and comfort in human-robot interactions.

← Back to Projects

The Problem: Why Human-Aware Navigation Matters

As robots transition from traditional industrial settings to public spaces—hospitals, restaurants, airports, shopping centers—navigation becomes far more complex. Traditional obstacle avoidance is no longer sufficient. Robots must now account for human comfort, safety, and social norms.

Imagine a delivery robot navigating a crowded hospital hallway. It's not enough to avoid collisions. The robot must respect personal space, avoid sudden movements in front of people, and understand social conventions about where it's acceptable to move.

What is Human-Aware Navigation (HAN)?

Human-Aware Navigation is a field of robotics that enables autonomous systems to navigate intelligently around humans by incorporating human behavioral factors, comfort metrics, and safety constraints into path planning.

HAN encompasses multiple sub-fields including perception, motion prediction, and human-robot interaction psychology. However, my research focuses specifically on the path planning aspect—developing a foundational framework where human-related behavioral rules can be mathematically modeled and optimized.

Core HAN Rules in My Research

While hundreds of HAN rules exist depending on context and environment, my work focuses on three fundamental principles:

  • Proxemics (PR): A safety buffer around humans respecting personal space and comfort. Violated when the robot enters this zone.
  • Back Space (BS): A zone behind humans that robots should avoid. Humans can't see behind them, making sudden robot movements unsafe.
  • Field of View (FOV): The visible area in front of humans. Robots should avoid lingering here to remain clear when humans move.

Important: These dimensions vary based on context. Older humans may require larger personal space buffers. Groups of humans have different dynamics than individuals.

Technical Approach: Enhanced Potential Field + Fuzzy Logic

1. Why Potential Field-Based Planning?

I selected potential field methods for their mathematical elegance. The concept is intuitive:

  • The robot is attracted to the goal (like north pole magnets)
  • Obstacles and humans repel the robot (like south poles)
  • The net effect creates a safe, smooth path to the destination

2. The Problem with Standard APF

Traditional Artificial Potential Fields (APF) suffer from two critical issues:

  • Local Minima: The robot gets stuck in suboptimal positions, unable to progress toward the goal
  • Goal Not Reachable (GNRON): When obstacles are too close to the goal, the robot cannot approach it

3. My Solution: Enhanced Potential Field (EPF)

I developed modifications to standard APF that address these limitations:

  • Rotate repulsive fields generated by obstacles by a calculated degree to escape local minima
  • Account for distance-to-goal in repulsive field calculations to solve GNRON

This solves the mathematical problems, but introduces a new challenge: parameter tuning. The EPF alone requires manual adjustment for each new scenario.

4. Adaptive Control with Fuzzy Inference System (FIS)

To make the system adaptive, I integrated a Fuzzy Inference System that dynamically adjusts the scaling factors of the potential field based on the environment:

  • What FIS does: Uses fuzzy logic to handle uncertainty and make human-like decisions
  • Why it works: Instead of rigid rules, FIS uses degrees of truth to map environmental states to optimal parameters
  • Example: "If far from goal and no obstacles nearby, increase attractive force for speed"

Research Results: Three Test Scenarios

Scenario 1: Simple Environment

Objective: Validate that EPF+FIS can overcome local minima and GNRON in a controlled setting with one human and static obstacles.

Results: Robot successfully navigated to goal while maintaining safe distance from human and obstacles. No local minima or GNRON issues observed.

Scenario 2: Moderate Complexity

Objective: Test in a more realistic environment with multiple obstacles and multiple humans.

Setup: 3 static obstacles, 2 humans with different proxemics requirements, multiple waypoints.

Results: System maintained all HAN constraints while navigating complex interactions between obstacles and humans.

Scenario 3: Real-World Airport Environment

Objective: Validate robustness in a high-complexity, realistic setting.

Setup: Modeled a busy airport section with 6 humans, 2 large circular obstacles, 2 rectangular obstacles, multiple navigation constraints.

Results: Robot successfully navigated the complex, crowded environment while respecting all HAN rules. Demonstrates scalability and real-world applicability.

Key Findings & Interpretation

The FIS-assisted EPF approach successfully:

  • Adaptively selects optimal parameters for diverse environments
  • Maintains all human comfort and safety constraints
  • Scales from simple to complex scenarios
  • Produces smooth, human-like navigation paths

Future Research Directions

This work opens multiple avenues for future exploration:

  • Robustness & Scalability: Optimize FIS parameters across diverse environments. Currently tuned intuitively.
  • Extended HAN Rules: Incorporate additional rules beyond proxemics (cultural norms, group dynamics, accessibility needs)
  • Vision & Prediction: Integrate computer vision and human motion prediction for reactive planning
  • Real-World Deployment: Test in actual public spaces with real humans and unpredictable scenarios
  • Multi-Agent Systems: Extend to multiple robots navigating in shared human spaces
